SECTION 1: PROJECT OVERVIEW
================================================================================

What is this project about?

I built a complete sales data analysis project using a real-world dataset called
"Global Superstore" — a retail dataset with 51,290 orders spanning 4 years
(2011 to 2014). The data covers 7 global markets (US, Europe, Asia-Pacific,
Latin America, Africa, EMEA, and Canada) across 3 product categories
(Technology, Furniture, and Office Supplies).

The goal was to answer key business questions:
  - Which products and categories make the most money?
  - Which markets and regions are strongest?
  - Are discounts helping or hurting our profits?
  - What seasonal patterns exist in sales?
  - Who are our most valuable customers?

I used three tools — SQL for querying, Python for analysis and visualization,
and Power BI for building an interactive dashboard.

SECTION 4: KEY FINDINGS (Top 7 Insights with Numbers)
================================================================================

1. STRONG REVENUE GROWTH
   - Revenue grew 90.3% from 2011 to 2014
   - 2011: $2.26M → 2014: $4.30M
   - Consistent year-over-year growth: 18.5%, 27.2%, 26.3%

2. TECHNOLOGY IS THE TOP CATEGORY
   - Technology led with $4.74M in sales and 14.0% profit margin
   - Office Supplies had similar margin (13.7%) but lower sales ($3.79M)
   - Furniture had the weakest margin at only 6.9%

3. TABLES ARE LOSING MONEY
   - Tables sub-category was the ONLY one losing money
   - This is a critical finding — the company should investigate pricing,
     costs, and discount strategy for tables

4. DISCOUNTS ABOVE 30% DESTROY PROFIT
   - Orders with no discount averaged $61.04 profit
   - Orders with >30% discount averaged -$76.59 (a LOSS)
   - Correlation between discount and profit: -0.316
   - Recommendation: Cap discounts at 20% to protect margins

5. ASIA-PACIFIC IS THE LARGEST MARKET
   - APAC: $3.59M (28.4% of total revenue)
   - EU: $2.94M (23.2%)
   - US: $2.30M (18.2%)
   - Canada is tiny at only $67K (0.5%)

6. SEASONALITY — Q4 IS PEAK SEASON
   - December, November, and September are the top 3 sales months
   - February is the slowest month
   - Recommendation: Increase inventory and marketing spend in Q4

7. CONSUMER SEGMENT DOMINATES
   - Consumer: 51.5% of sales
   - Corporate: 30.3%
   - Home Office: 18.3%

SECTION 5: BUSINESS RECOMMENDATIONS
================================================================================

Based on the analysis, here are my recommendations:

1. STOP HEAVY DISCOUNTING
   Discounts over 30% cause net losses. Implement a discount cap policy
   at 20% maximum. Instead of blanket discounts, use targeted promotions.

2. FIX THE TABLES SUB-CATEGORY
   Tables are the only loss-making sub-category. Investigate whether the
   issue is pricing, supplier costs, or excessive discounting.

3. INVEST IN THE APAC MARKET
   APAC is the largest and a growing market. Allocate more marketing budget
   and inventory to this region.

4. LEVERAGE SEASONAL PATTERNS
   Build inventory ahead of September-December (peak season).
   Run clearance sales in January-February (slow season).

5. FOCUS ON TECHNOLOGY PRODUCTS
   Technology has the highest margin AND highest revenue.
   Expand the technology product line, especially Copiers and Phones
   (the most profitable sub-categories).

6. IMPROVE FURNITURE MARGINS
   Furniture sales are strong ($4.1M) but margins are only 6.9%.
   Review supplier pricing and reduce discounts to improve profitability.

SECTION 6: COMMON INTERVIEW QUESTIONS & ANSWERS
================================================================================

Q1: What was the business problem you were trying to solve?
A: The company had 4 years of sales data but no clear insights into which
   products, markets, and strategies were driving profitability. I analyzed
   the data to identify trends, unprofitable segments, and opportunities for
   growth. The key finding was that heavy discounts (>30%) were destroying
   profits, and the Tables sub-category was losing money.

Q2: How did you collect and clean the data?
A: The data came from a Global Superstore CSV file with 51,290 rows and
   24 columns. Cleaning steps included: parsing date columns into proper
   date formats, handling null Postal Codes (expected for non-US markets),
   and deriving new columns like Year, Month, Quarter, and Profit Margin.
   The data was mostly clean with no duplicate or corrupted rows.

Q3: What tools did you use and why?
A: I used SQL for structured queries (industry standard for data extraction),
   Python with Pandas for data manipulation and Matplotlib for visualizations
   (gives the most control over chart formatting), and Power BI for building
   an interactive dashboard (most popular BI tool in business environments).

Q4: What was the most interesting finding?
A: The discount-profit relationship was the most actionable finding. Orders
   with no discount averaged $61 profit, but orders with >30% discount
   averaged -$77 (a net loss). The negative correlation (-0.316) proved
   that the company's discounting strategy was hurting overall profitability.
   I recommended capping discounts at 20%.

Q5: How would you explain your methodology to a non-technical person?
A: I looked at four years of sales data for a global retailer. First, I
   organized the data using SQL to answer specific business questions like
   "which products make the most money?" Then I created visual charts using
   Python to make the patterns easy to see. Finally, I designed a dashboard
   in Power BI so business leaders can explore the data themselves. The
   analysis revealed that aggressive discounting was costing the company
   money, and certain product categories needed pricing adjustments.

Q6: How did you decide which metrics to analyze?
A: I focused on metrics that directly impact business decisions:
   - Revenue and Profit (how much money are we making?)
   - Profit Margin (how efficient is each category/market?)
   - YoY Growth (are we improving over time?)
   - Discount Impact (are promotions actually helping?)
   - Customer Segmentation (who are our best customers?)
   These metrics align with what a Business Analyst would track in a real
   retail environment.

Q7: What was the biggest challenge you faced?
A: The biggest challenge was making the analysis actionable rather than
   just descriptive. Anyone can say "Technology sells the most," but the
   real value was in discovering that Tables lose money, that discounts
   above 30% cause losses, and that APAC has the most growth potential.
   I focused on insights that could directly inform business decisions.

Q8: If you had more time, what would you add?
A: I would add:
   - Customer lifetime value (CLV) analysis to identify at-risk customers
   - Predictive forecasting using time-series models (ARIMA/Prophet)
   - RFM analysis (Recency, Frequency, Monetary) for customer segmentation
   - A/B testing framework for discount strategies
   - Cohort analysis to track customer retention over time

Q9: How did you ensure your analysis was accurate?
A: I cross-validated results across tools — the SQL queries, Python analysis,
   and Power BI dashboard all produced matching numbers. I also checked for
   data quality issues like duplicates, nulls, and outliers before running
   any analysis. The profit margins and growth rates were verified manually
   for a subset of the data.

Q10: What would you recommend to the business based on your findings?
A: My top three recommendations are:
   1. Cap discounts at 20% — discounts above 30% create losses
   2. Investigate the Tables sub-category — it's the only one losing money
   3. Invest more in APAC market — it's the largest (28.4%) and growing
   These three actions alone could significantly improve profitability.
